Recommendation memo: a second tool-lending pickup site

Situation

The neighborhood tool-lending cooperative currently serves members from one small storefront. Weekend reservations have increased, and thirty-two members from the eastern service area reported that the forty-minute round trip discourages short rentals. The cooperative has a limited reserve, six regular volunteers, and no budget for another permanent lease this year. The decision is whether to keep one site, add delivery, or test a smaller pickup point closer to those members.

Recommendation

The cooperative should run a twelve-week pop-up pickup site in the unused meeting room of the eastern community center. The site would open Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday afternoons and would hold only items reserved online by noon the previous day. A reversible pilot addresses the access problem without committing the reserve to a second lease. Continuation should depend on at least forty completed pickups, fewer than five inventory-transfer errors, and survey evidence that the location reduced travel barriers.

Operating plan

Reserved items would be packed at the main site each morning, moved in labeled bins, and checked back into the main inventory after the pop-up closes. No additional paid shift or volunteer recruitment period is included in the pilot budget.

Tradeoffs

Restricting the pop-up to advance reservations reduces spontaneity, but it keeps the satellite inventory small and makes missing-item checks practical. The plan also accepts two weekly transfer trips in exchange for avoiding a permanent duplicate stock. If transfers regularly delay opening or remove popular items from the main storefront, the cooperative should reduce the pop-up schedule rather than hide that cost.

Rejected alternatives

Home delivery was rejected because a driver route would require vehicle insurance, address handling, and unpredictable travel time for each rental. A full second storefront was also rejected for now because rent and duplicate storage would consume most of the reserve before demand at the new location is demonstrated. Keeping one site would protect the reserve but would leave the documented travel barrier unchanged.

Risks and review

The largest operational risk is that moving reserved tools creates errors between the online record and the physical bins. A two-person transfer checklist and a separate pop-up shelf in the inventory system should reduce that risk. The pilot review should report pickup volume, transfer errors, volunteer hours, and missed openings. If the evidence shows reliable demand and manageable operations, the board can compare a longer community-center agreement with a delivery partnership. If not, the cooperative can end the pilot without a lease obligation.

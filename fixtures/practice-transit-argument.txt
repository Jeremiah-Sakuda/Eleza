A city should convert one lane of its downtown ring road into a bus-only corridor because reliable public transport moves more people through limited street space. The proposal is not a claim that every resident can stop driving. It is a claim about how a crowded center should use its most constrained public resource.

The first benefit is predictable travel time. A bus trapped in mixed traffic offers little advantage over a car, so riders cannot confidently plan school, work, or care appointments. A dedicated lane removes much of that uncertainty. Even when the bus is not faster at every hour, a narrower range of arrival times makes the service more useful.

Critics argue that removing a general-traffic lane will create congestion on nearby streets. Some displacement is likely, especially during the first months. The city should therefore change signal timing, mark delivery windows, and measure traffic on parallel routes. That qualification does not defeat the proposal; it identifies the management work required to make it succeed.

The corridor should also be evaluated as a public-space decision, not only a transport experiment. Fewer cars moving through the center would reduce noise and make crossings easier, while more reliable buses would improve access for people who cannot drive. A one-year pilot with published monthly data would let residents test those benefits against the predicted costs before the lane becomes permanent.

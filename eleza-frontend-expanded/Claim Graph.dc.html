Movable type and the standardization of vernacular English
Student S-0417 · 2,340 words · parsed 14 July 2026

English spelling did not settle because anyone decided that it should. It settled because print shops made variation expensive: compositors were paid by the page, resetting type cost money, and a house style was cheaper than a standing argument at the composing stone.

A century before any academy existed, the shops were already acting as de facto orthographic authorities. If Wynkyn de Worde’s house printed booke one way for twenty years, that quietly became how the word was spelled; there was no institution to appeal to.

The harder case is Caxton. His Chancery spellings spread because of print, not because of the court’s prestige. The prestige account predicts official documents leading the change; instead, private correspondence shows the printed forms within a generation (Fisher, ch. 2), well before official usage converged.

The convergence itself is measurable. Analysis of ligature frequencies across fourteen London print shops shows orthographic convergence by 1523 — decades before any schoolmaster codified a rule.
